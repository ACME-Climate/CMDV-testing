First-Order $x-y$ Stokes MMS Test Cases¶

Component Being Tested¶

Albany Land-Ice (ALI) model, First-Order Stokes stress-velocity solver [5]. This model is included in the land-ice components of the E3SM, and is implemented within the Albany code base [4], available on github at the following URL: https://github.com/SNLComputation/Albany.

Description¶

It is commontly accepted the land ice behaves like a power-law viscous, incompressible fluid in a low Reynolds number flow, described by the nonlinear Stokes flow equations for glaciers and ice sheets [1-2]. Here, we consider the First-Order (FO) approximation to these equations, also referred to as the Blatter–Pattyn model [3-4]. This model is derived from the full Stokes model under the assumption of a small geometric aspect ratio, and the assumption that the normal vectors to the ice sheet’s upper and lower surfaces are nearly vertical.

3D First-Order Stokes Equations¶

The First-Order (FO) Stokes equations [3-4] are given by: $$ \begin{equation} \left\{\begin{array}{rcl} -\nabla \cdot (2 \mu \dot{{\mathbf{ \epsilon}}}_1) &=& -\rho g \frac{\partial s}{\partial x}, \\ -\nabla \cdot (2 \mu \dot{{\mathbf{\epsilon}}}_2) &=& -\rho g \frac{\partial s}{\partial y} \\ \end{array}\right.\tag{1} \end{equation} $$

Here, in the most general (3D) case,

$$ \begin{equation} \dot{{\mathbf{\epsilon}}}_1^T = \left(\begin{array}{ccc} 2\dot{\epsilon}_{xx} + \dot{\epsilon}_{yy}, &\dot{\epsilon}_{xy},& \dot{\epsilon}_{xz}\end{array}\right) \tag{2} \end{equation} $$$$ \begin{equation} \dot{{\mathbf{\epsilon}}}_2^T = \left( \begin{array}{ccc}\dot{\epsilon}_{xy}, & \dot{\epsilon}_{xx} + 2\dot{\epsilon}_{yy}, &\dot{\epsilon}_{yz} \end{array}\right) \tag{3} \end{equation} $$$$ \begin{equation} \dot{\epsilon}_{ij} = \frac{1}{2}\left( \frac{\partial u_i}{\partial x_j} + \frac{\partial u_j}{\partial x_i}\right) \end{equation}\tag{4} $$

for $i,j \in \{ x,y,z\}$. The viscosity $\mu$ is given by Glen's law:

$$ \begin{equation} \mu = \frac{1}{2} A^{-\frac{1}{n}} \left(\dot{\epsilon}_{xx}^2 + \dot{\epsilon}_{yy}^2 + \dot{\epsilon}_{xx} \dot{\epsilon}_{yy} + \dot{\epsilon}_{xy}^2 + \dot{\epsilon}_{xz}^2 + \dot{\epsilon}_{yz}^2 \right)^{\left( \frac{1}{2n}-\frac{1}{2}\right)} \tag{5} \end{equation} $$

Here, $A$ is the flow rate factor, and $n$ is the Glen's law exponent ($n=3$ for ice sheets).

2D $x-y$ First-Order Stokes Equations¶

The MMS problems given in this document are for a simplified version of the first order Stokes equations, namely (1) in two spatial dimensions (2D) and on a rectangular geometry aligned with the $x$- and $y$-axes: $\Omega = (0,1) \times (0,1)$ [6]. For this geometry, $s(x,y) = const$, so the gravity-based source terms in (1) vanish. Some additional sources $f_1$ and $f_2$ are added to the equations, however. Toward this effect, the governing equations for the MMS problems presented here are the following:

$$ \begin{equation} \left\{\begin{array}{rcl} -\nabla \cdot (2 \mu \dot{{\mathbf{ \epsilon}}}_1) +f_1&=& 0, \\ -\nabla \cdot (2 \mu \dot{{\mathbf{\epsilon}}}_2) + f_2&=& 0\\ \end{array}\right. \tag{6} \end{equation} $$

where

$$ \begin{equation} \dot{{\mathbf{\epsilon}}}_1^T = \left(\begin{array}{cc} 2\dot{\epsilon}_{xx} + \dot{\epsilon}_{yy}, &\dot{\epsilon}_{xy}\end{array}\right) \tag{7} \end{equation} $$$$ \begin{equation} \dot{{\mathbf{\epsilon}}}_2^T = \left( \begin{array}{cc}\dot{\epsilon}_{xy}, & \dot{\epsilon}_{xx} + 2\dot{\epsilon}_{yy} \end{array}\right) \tag{8} \end{equation} $$

and

$$ \begin{equation} \mu = \frac{1}{2} A^{-\frac{1}{n}} \left(\dot{\epsilon}_{xx}^2 + \dot{\epsilon}_{yy}^2 + \dot{\epsilon}_{xx} \dot{\epsilon}_{yy} + \dot{\epsilon}_{xy}^2 \right)^{\left( \frac{1}{2n}-\frac{1}{2}\right)} \tag{9} \end{equation} $$

with $\dot{\epsilon}_{ij}$ given by (4). Written out, (6) has the form:

$$ \begin{equation} \left\{\begin{array}{rcl} -\frac{\partial}{\partial x} \left( 4 \mu \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + 2\mu \frac{\partial v}{\partial y}\right) - \frac{\partial}{\partial y} \left( \mu \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} + \mu \frac{\partial v}{\partial x}\right) + f_1&=& 0, \\ -\frac{\partial}{\partial x} \left( \mu \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + \mu \frac{\partial v}{\partial y}\right) - \frac{\partial}{\partial y} \left(2\mu \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + 4\mu \frac{\partial v}{\partial y} \right) +f_2 &=& 0\\ \end{array}\right. \tag{10} \end{equation} $$

SinCos2D MMS Test Case¶

The first MMS test case derived will be referred to as the Sincos2D test case. It is an Dirichlet/homogeneous Neumann BVP on the unit square: $\Omega = (0,1)\times (0,1)$. The force terms in (6) and boundary conditions are derived such that the exact solution to the problem is given by:

$$ \begin{equation} \label{stokes2Dsol} \begin{array}{rcl} u &= & \sin(2 \pi x + \phi) \cos(2\pi y + \psi) + 3\pi x \\ v &=& -\cos(2\pi x + \phi) \sin(2\pi y + \psi) - 3 \pi y \end{array} \tag{11} \end{equation} $$

The parameters $\phi, \psi \in [0, 2\pi)$ in (11) are phase shifts that can be used to generate a family of solutions. In order to obtain this exact solution, the source terms in (6) are given by:

$$ \begin{equation} f_1 = -8\mu \pi^2 \sin(2\pi x + \phi)\cos(2\pi y + \psi) + A^{-\frac{1}{n}} \left(\frac{1}{n} - 1\right)\tilde{\mu}^{\frac{1}{n} - 2}\left(\frac{\partial \tilde{\mu}}{\partial x}(2\epsilon_{xx} + \epsilon_{yy}) + \frac{\partial \tilde{\mu}}{\partial y}\epsilon_{xy}\right) \tag{12} \end{equation} $$$$ \begin{equation} f_2 = 8\mu\pi^2\cos(2\pi x + \phi)\sin(2\pi y + \psi) + A^{-\frac{1}{n}} \left(\frac{1}{n} - 1\right)\tilde{\mu}^{\frac{1}{n} - 2}\left(\frac{\partial \tilde{\mu}}{\partial x} \epsilon_{xy} + \frac{\partial \tilde{\mu}}{\partial y}(\epsilon_{xx} + 2\epsilon_{yy})\right) \tag{13} \end{equation} $$

where

$$ \begin{equation} \tilde{\mu} = 2\pi\cos(2\pi x + \phi)\cos(2\pi y + \psi) +3\pi x \tag{14} \end{equation} $$$$ \begin{equation} \frac{\partial \tilde{\mu} }{\partial x} = -4\pi^2\sin(2\pi x + \phi)\cos(2\pi y + \psi) \tag{15} \end{equation} $$$$ \begin{equation} \frac{\partial \tilde{\mu}}{\partial y} = -4\pi^2\cos(2\pi x + \phi)\sin(2\pi y + \psi) \tag{16} \end{equation} $$$$ \begin{equation} \epsilon_{xx} = 2\pi\cos(2\pi x + \phi)\cos(2\pi y + \psi) + 3\pi \tag{17} \end{equation} $$$$ \begin{equation} \epsilon_{yy} = -2\pi\cos(2\pi x + \phi)\cos(2\pi y + \psi) - 3\pi \tag{18} \end{equation} $$$$ \begin{equation} \epsilon_{xy} = 0 \tag{19} \end{equation} $$$$ \begin{equation} \mu = \frac{1}{2}A^{-\frac{1}{n}} \tilde{\mu}^{\frac{1}{n} - 1} \tag{20} \end{equation} $$

The following piece of code specifies $f_1$ and $f_2$ (taken from the Albany code base):

       double r = 3.0*pi;
       MeshScalarT x2pi = 2.0*pi*coordVec(cell,qp,0);
       MeshScalarT y2pi = 2.0*pi*coordVec(cell,qp,1);
       MeshScalarT muargt = 2.0*pi*cos(x2pi + xphase)*cos(y2pi + yphase) + r;
       MeshScalarT muqp = 0.5*pow(A, -1.0/n)*pow(muargt, 1.0/n - 1.0);
       MeshScalarT dmuargtdx = -4.0*pi*pi*sin(x2pi + xphase)*cos(y2pi + yphase);
       MeshScalarT dmuargtdy = -4.0*pi*pi*cos(x2pi + xphase)*sin(y2pi + yphase);
       MeshScalarT exx = 2.0*pi*cos(x2pi + xphase)*cos(y2pi + yphase) + r;
       MeshScalarT eyy = -2.0*pi*cos(x2pi + xphase)*cos(y2pi + yphase) - r;
       MeshScalarT exy = 0.0;
       f[0] = 2.0*muqp*(-4.0*pi*pi*sin(x2pi + xphase)*cos(y2pi + yphase))
            + 2.0*0.5*pow(A, -1.0/n)*(1.0/n - 1.0)*pow(muargt, 1.0/n
            - 2.0)*(dmuargtdx*(2.0*exx + eyy) + dmuargtdy*exy);
       f[1] = 2.0*muqp*(4.0*pi*pi*cos(x2pi + xphase)*sin(y2pi + yphase))
            + 2.0*0.5*pow(A, -1.0/n)*(1.0/n - 1.0)*pow(muargt, 1.0/n
            - 2.0)*(dmuargtdx*exy + dmuargtdy*(exx + 2.0*eyy));

To finish the problem specification, we still need to give the boundary conditions. They are:

$$ \begin{equation} \begin{array}{rcll} u&=& 0, & \text{at } x=0 \\ u &=& 3\pi, & \text{at } x = 1\\ 2\mu \dot{{\bf{ \epsilon}}}_2 \cdot \mathbf{n} &=& 0, & \text{at } y=0, y=1 \\ 2\mu \dot{{\bf{\epsilon}}}_1 \cdot \mathbf{n} &=& 0, & \text{at } x=0, x=1\\ v &=& 0, & \text{at } y = 0 \\ v &=& -3\pi, & \text{at } y = 1 \end{array} \tag{21} \end{equation} $$

Note that the Sincos2Dtest case can be turned into a constant viscosity test case by setting $n = 1$.

Variable Definitions¶

The input file for the SinCos2D test case is named input_fo_sincos2DT.yaml and can be found in the tests/small/LandIce/FO_MMS directory of the Albany code. Here, the following parameter values are utilized:

$$ \begin{array}{l|l} \text{Variable} & \text{Value} \\ \hline n & 3 \\ A & 1.0 \\ \phi & 0.0 \\ \psi & 0.0 \\ \end{array} $$

CosExp2D MMS Test Case¶

The second MMS test case derived will be referred to as the CosExp2D test case, and is detailed in [6]. The problem specification includes Robin boundary conditions resembling the basal sliding boundary condition. For this problem, the force terms in (6) and boundary conditions are derived such that the exact solution to the problem is given by:

$$ \begin{equation} \begin{array}{rcl} u &= & e^x \sin(2\pi y) \\ v &=& e^x \cos(2 \pi y) \end{array} \tag{22} \end{equation} $$

In order to obtain this exact solution, the source terms in (6) are given by:

$$ \begin{equation} f_1 = 2\mu\exp(x)\sin(2\pi y)\left[2 - 3\pi - 2\pi^2\right] + A^{-\frac{1}{n}}\left(\frac{1}{n}-1\right)\tilde{\mu}^{ \frac{1}{n}-2}\left(\frac{\partial \tilde{\mu}}{\partial x}(2\epsilon_{xx} + \epsilon_{yy}) + \frac{\partial \tilde{\mu}}{\partial y}\epsilon_{xy}\right) \tag{23} \end{equation} $$$$ \begin{equation} f_2 = 2\mu\exp(x)\cos(2\pi y)\left[3\pi + \frac{1}{2} - 8\pi^2\right] + A^{-\frac{1}{n}}\left(\frac{1}{n}-1\right)\tilde{\mu}^{\frac{1}{n}-2} \left(\frac{\partial \tilde{\mu}}{\partial x}\epsilon_{xy} + \frac{\partial \tilde{\mu}}{\partial y}(\epsilon_{xx} + 2\epsilon_{yy})\right) \tag{24} \end{equation} $$

where

$$ \begin{equation} \tilde{\mu} = \exp(x) \sqrt{(1 + 4\pi^2 - 2\pi)\sin^2(2\pi y) + \frac{1}{4}(2\pi+1)^2\cos^2(2\pi y)} \tag{25} \end{equation} $$$$ \begin{equation} \frac{\partial \tilde{\mu} }{\partial x} = \tilde{\mu} \tag{26} \end{equation} $$$$ \begin{equation} \frac{\partial \tilde{\mu}}{\partial y} = \frac{3}{2}\frac{\pi(1+4\pi^2-4\pi)\cos(2\pi y)\sin(2\pi y)\exp(x)}{\sqrt{(1 + 4\pi^2 - 2\pi)\sin^2(2\pi y) + \frac{1}{4}(2\pi+1)^2\cos^2(2\pi y)}} \tag{27} \end{equation} $$$$ \begin{equation} \epsilon_{xx} = \exp(x)\sin(2\pi y) \tag{28} \end{equation} $$$$ \begin{equation} \epsilon_{yy} = -2\pi\exp(x)\sin(2\pi y) \tag{29} \end{equation} $$$$ \begin{equation} \epsilon_{xy} = \frac{1}{2}(2\pi+1)\exp(x)\cos(2\pi y) \tag{30} \end{equation} $$$$ \begin{equation} \mu = \frac{1}{2}A^{-\frac{1}{n}} \tilde{\mu}^{\frac{1}{n} - 1} \tag{31} \end{equation} $$

The following piece of code specifies $f_1$ and $f_2$ (taken from the Albany code base):

       double a = 1.0;
       MeshScalarT x2pi = 2.0*pi*coordVec(cell,qp,0);
       MeshScalarT x = coordVec(cell,qp,0);
       MeshScalarT y2pi = 2.0*pi*coordVec(cell,qp,1);
       MeshScalarT muargt = (a*a + 4.0*pi*pi - 2.0*pi*a)*sin(y2pi)*sin(y2pi)
                          + 1.0/4.0*(2.0*pi+a)*(2.0*pi+a)*cos(y2pi)*cos(y2pi);
       muargt = sqrt(muargt)*exp(a*x);
       MeshScalarT muqp = 1.0/2.0*pow(A, -1.0/n)*pow(muargt, 1.0/n - 1.0);
       MeshScalarT dmuargtdx = a*muargt;
       MeshScalarT dmuargtdy = 3.0/2.0*pi*(a*a+4.0*pi*pi
                             -4.0*pi*a)*cos(y2pi)*sin(y2pi)*exp(a*x)/sqrt((a*a
                             + 4.0*pi*pi - 2.0*pi*a)*sin(y2pi)*sin(y2pi)
                             + 1.0/4.0*(2.0*pi+a)*(2.0*pi+a)*cos(y2pi)*cos(y2pi));
       MeshScalarT exx = a*exp(a*x)*sin(y2pi);
       MeshScalarT eyy = -2.0*pi*exp(a*x)*sin(y2pi);
       MeshScalarT exy = 1.0/2.0*(2.0*pi+a)*exp(a*x)*cos(y2pi);
       f[0] = 2.0*muqp*(2.0*a*a*exp(a*x)*sin(y2pi) - 3.0*pi*a*exp(a*x)*sin(y2pi)
            - 2.0*pi*pi*exp(a*x)*sin(y2pi))
            + 2.0*0.5*pow(A, -1.0/n)*(1.0/n-1.0)*pow(muargt, 1.0/n-2.0)*(dmuargtdx*(2.0*exx
            + eyy) + dmuargtdy*exy);
       f[1] = 2.0*muqp*(3.0*a*pi*exp(a*x)*cos(y2pi) + 1.0/2.0*a*a*exp(a*x)*cos(y2pi)
            - 8.0*pi*pi*exp(a*x)*cos(y2pi)) + 2.0*0.5*pow(A, -1.0/n)*(1.0/n-1.0)*pow(muargt,
            1.0/n-2.0)*(dmuargtdx*exy + dmuargtdy*(exx + 2.0*eyy));

To finish the problem specification, we still need to give the boundary conditions. They are:

$$ \begin{equation} \begin{array}{rcll} u &=& 0, & \text{at } y=0, 1 \\ v &=& 0, & \text{at } (x,y) = (0,0) \\ 2\mu \dot{{\mathbf{ \epsilon}}}_1 \cdot \mathbf{n} &=& 4(\pi-1)\mu u, & \text{at } x=0 \\ 2\mu \dot{{\mathbf{ \epsilon}}}_1 \cdot \mathbf{n} &=& -4(\pi -1) \mu u, & \text{at } x=1 \\ 2\mu \dot{{\mathbf{ \epsilon}}}_2 \cdot \mathbf{n} &=& -(2\pi + 1) v, & \text{at } x=0\\ 2\mu \dot{{\mathbf{ \epsilon}}}_2 \cdot \mathbf{n} &=& (2\pi + 1) v, & \text{at } x=1\\ 2\mu \dot{{\mathbf{ \epsilon}}}_2 \cdot \mathbf{n} &=& 0, & \text{at } y=0, 1 \end{array} \tag{32} \end{equation} $$

The boundary condition at the single point $(x,y) = (0,0)$ for $v$ is specified to make $v$ unique (otherwise, $v$ could be non-unique up to a constant).

Note that the CosExp2D test case can be turned into a constant viscosity test case by setting $n = 1$.
